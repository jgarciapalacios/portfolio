Proposition: NYC police disproportionately use excessive force against communities of color.

FOR the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • Mapping Population Demographics by Precinct (Earnestness Score: +1.5)
    The map is color-coded by the majority racial group in each precinct (given by the US Census Bureau), allowing readers to easily identify where communities of color are concentrated. By overlaying complaint data on top, the visualization naturally highlights disparities in excessive force complaints without needing additional explanation. One small critique might be that diverse communites may have only a slight a majority, thereby making the coloring of the whole precinct one color misleading; however, in practice there are very few, if any, communities like this. Overall, this was just the natural choice.
  • Using Blue Opaque Circles to Represent the Magnitude of Complaints for each Precinct (Earnestness Score: -1)
    The blue circles cluster around communities of color, particularly Black and Hispanic-majority precincts. This visually reinforces the argument that these communities experience more excessive force complaints. Blue is also associated with police, making the meaning of the circles intuitive. However, the circles are misleading considering the fact that the precincts with the largest circles are also the ones with the most complaints. In fact, if you subsituted execessive force for any other complaint allegation, the chart would've looked exactly the same, making this graph extremely misleading. For an alternative, I did think of using barcharts to overlay on the map for each precinct, but it would have quickly got messy as it would cover up precinct numbers and colors.
  • Zooming in on Precinct 75 for Additional Context (Earnestness Score: -.5)
    Precinct 75 stands out as having one of the highest numbers of excessive force complaints. Naturally, readers will be curious as to why the circle is so large. Therefore, by zooming in, I suggest to readers that they do not need to go do their own research about precinct 75 because I have the data for them, allowing me to set the narrative. Once they're zoomed in; I show the demographics of precinct 75 next to that of nyc more generally, suggesting that its the difference in demographics that's the reason for the high number of complaints. Although not motivated by the best intentions, the fact is that there is still a huge discrepency between precinct 75 and other precincts. I think it's valid to suggest that the difference in demographics is at least part of the reason. A possible alternative was to compare multiple precincts side by side, but focusing on one strong example is more compelling.
  • Comparing Substantiation Rates with a Side-by-Side View (Earnestness Score: +1)
    Displaying both the citywide substantiation rate (12.7%) and Precinct 75’s lower rate (7.5%) was a deliberate choice. While some may argue that communities of color are simply more likely to be in situations where excessive force is necessary, the caption intentionally frames the issue as complaints being dismissed. Using the word "dismissed" shifts the focus from potential justification of force to a lack of police accountability.
    Now with this framing, the bolded text in the caption highlights that communities of color are twice as likely to have their complaints dismissed, driving the point home that communites of color are disproportionately affected, even after the actual altercation. Originally, I considered framing the comparison as "92.5% dismissal rate vs. 87.3% dismissal rate," but this lacked the same impact. The numbers 7.5% vs. 12.7% create a much starker contrast—12.7% feels significantly larger than 7.5%, whereas 92% and 87% seem much closer together. Using the lower absolute values makes the difference feel more dramatic and reinforces the disparity in substantiation rates more effectively.
    While this comparison is valid and revealing, it is based on a single case study, which could be seen as misleading.

AGAINST the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • Using a Y-axis Scale of 100% to Minimize Differences (Earnestness Score: +1)
    Although most of the data falls below 20%, I chose to set the Y-axis scale to 100%. This decision visually compresses the differences between racial groups, making any disparities in substantiation rates appear smaller. By reducing visual contrast, the argument that excessive force disproportionately affects communities of color becomes less immediately apparent. However, any other choice could be argued to be just as misleading.
  • Applying a 3-Year Rolling Average to Smooth Out Major Jumps (Earnestness Score: -1)
    Sudden spikes in substantiation rates could indicate meaningful shifts in how complaints are handled, but applying a 3-year rolling average helps neutralize those changes. This ensures no single moment stands out, making it harder for readers to point to instances where racial disparities might have been more extreme. The result is a visualization that suggests subtle, rather than dramatic, differences over time.
  • Framing Substantiation Rate as a Response to Broader Events, Not Race (Earnestness Score: -2)
    Instead of emphasizing racial disparities, I structured the visualization around major U.S. events—such as Broken Windows Policing, 9/11 and Increased Security, and The Great Recession. This choice shifts the narrative away from race and towards external societal factors that influenced policing. For example, I could have chosen to include the death of Eric Garner in 2014 which sparked massive outcry for police reform; Instead I chose to include that Stop and Frisk ended the year earlier. Because substantiation rates seem to increase afterwards, readers are more likely to attribute better rates to the ending of Stop and Frisk. The takeaway is that substantiation rates are largely determined by historical context, not racial bias.
  • Including the Overall Substantiation Rate to Downplay Racial Differences (Earnestness Score: +0.5)
    By adding a general substantiation rate for all complaints, I reinforce the idea that excessive force complaints have low substantiation rates across the board, regardless of race. This can lead readers to conclude that any differences between racial groups are minor and likely due to statistical noise rather than systemic bias.

Final Reflection

Throughout this process, I found that my ability to create persuasive visualizations often came from understanding the opposing perspective just as well as the one I aimed to support. Interestingly, when I set out to support a proposition, I was often able to more clearly identify evidence against it, and vice versa. This suggests that effective data storytelling requires deep engagement with both sides of an argument—not just to counter opposing viewpoints, but to ensure that the visualization is well-reasoned and ethical.
